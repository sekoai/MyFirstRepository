From Goats to Garage

How 3 goats per year became a transport empire

The Goat Mechanic

At 13, Tumelo started apprenticing with the only mechanic in Manamela village. Unlike shop workers who earned cash, skilled labor like mechanics were traditionally paid in livestock - in Tumelo's case, 3 goats per year.

"I would walk 8km daily to the garage," Tumelo remembers. "The old Toyota pickup we worked on was my classroom. By 15, I could rebuild an engine blindfolded, but my bank account was empty - my wealth walked on four legs."

Those nine goats I earned became my first fleet. While others saw meat, I saw transport opportunities - each goat was a down payment on wheels.

The Livestock Conversion Strategy

At 16, Tumelo made his first vehicle acquisition through creative livestock trading:

  • Year 1: Traded 5 goats for a non-running 1985 Toyota Hilux
  • Year 2: Used 2 goats to buy spare parts, got the Hilux running
  • Year 3: 1 goat provided fuel for first month of taxi service

This unconventional approach let Tumelo enter the transport business without cash capital.

The Taxi Revolution

Tumelo's "Goat Taxi" service changed village transport:

Before

  • Weekly bus to Maseru
  • No emergency transport
  • Goods moved by donkey carts

After

  • Daily trips to town
  • 24/7 ambulance service
  • First parcel delivery system

Within two years, Tumelo was running three vehicles and employing two drivers.

The Big Break: Mining Contracts

Tumelo's transport business scaled dramatically when he secured contracts with nearby mines:

  1. Started with worker shuttle service (1 truck)
  2. Expanded to equipment transport (3 flatbeds)
  3. Now handles mine supply logistics (12-vehicle fleet)

Key to his success was maintaining village roots while growing the business:

  • Headquarters remains in Manamela
  • 80% staff from local villages
  • Still accepts goats as payment from farmers
