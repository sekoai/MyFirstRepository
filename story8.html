From Cattle to Culinary King

How 4 cattle per year built a restaurant empire

The Cattle Cook

At age 14, Thabo began working as a cattle herder in Manamela village, earning 4 cattle per year. While others saw livestock as wealth, Thabo saw ingredients. "I learned to cook over open fires while watching the herds," he recalls. "My secret? I traded one cow for cooking lessons from my grandmother."

Those cattle were my kitchen. Each animal I sold bought pots, spices, and eventually my first food cart. Meat from my own herd became my signature dishes.

The Meat-to-Meals Strategy

Thabo's path from herder to restaurateur:

Year 1
Traded 2 cattle for commercial cooking equipment
Year 3
Sold 3 cattle to open "Bakeng Sa Lihoho" (For the People) food stall
Year 5
Used remaining cattle as collateral for first restaurant

The Signature Dish That Changed Everything

Thabo's "Herd-to-Table" concept revolutionized Basotho cuisine:

Lesotho Fire Chicken

Free-range chicken with wild basil

Mountain Bread

Stone-ground sorghum baked in clay ovens

Moroho Ice Cream

Sweet corn and wild spinach dessert

Building a Food Empire

Today, Thabo's Khomo ea Poho (Fat Cow) brand includes:

4 Restaurants

From Maseru to Johannesburg

Catering Service

Serving weddings and corporate events

Cooking School

Teaching traditional Basotho cuisine

Farm-to-Table Before It Was Trendy

Thabo maintains traditional values in his modern business:

Ethical Sourcing

Buys directly from village herders

Seasonal Menus

Changes with grazing patterns

Community Meals

Free food for elders every Thursday

Recipes for Success

Thabo's advice for aspiring food entrepreneurs:

Old recipes contain generations of wisdom

Innovate Carefully

Modernize presentation, not flavors

Feed Your People

Success tastes better when shared
